    1. 1 2017-06-20T17:16:35+00:00 Religious World Travelers 6 plain 2017-07-11T21:53:14+00:00
      Missionary activity increased significantly in the early modern period, as churches raced across the globe to entrench their vision of “true” Christianity in new lands before their spiritual rivals. In the process, missionaries contributed to the process of colonization, both by increasing European knowledge of unfamiliar territories, cultures, and languages, and helping to impose European ways there.

      No group – not even the Dominicans or the Franciscans – adopted this world-traveling identity with more enthusiasm than the Jesuits, which embraced its role as the vanguard of Catholic expansion throughout the globe. The order produced a range of printed works that celebrated their efforts to bring Christianity to distant locations, which simultaneously brought these seemingly exotic peoples more fully into European consciousness. A typical example of this process is found on the title page of Five Books on the Christian Expedition Among the Chinese, which features a detailed map of the regions in China and Southeast Asia where the Jesuit missionaries operated in the 16th and 17th centuries.
      Missionaries actively worked to establish European culture in the  lands where they proselytized. Protestant missionaries in New England argued that there was no alternative to this linkage.  The Jesuits were more heavily involved in the program of colonization in Brazil than anywhere else in the world, adding the task of governing indigenous peoples to those of converting and educating them. The Jesuits enshrined this aspect of their ministry in Brazil in the frontispiece to the Life of the Venerable Father José de Anchieta, which shows Anchieta standing before a shining city, perhaps São Paolo, where he founded a church and mission.
