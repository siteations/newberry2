    1. 1 2017-06-20T15:47:44+00:00 Reconverting 7 plain 2017-07-07T21:52:45+00:00
      In an age where the official religion of a given territory could vary, it was not out of the realm of possibility for individuals to convert from one form of Christianity to another and back again. Printed works of the period offer evidence of this experience, practically unthinkable in the Middle Ages.
      One of the most famous cases of reconversion was the English playwright Ben Jonson, who converted to Catholicism while in prison on suspicion of murder in 1598. Though illegal at the time, Jonson’s Catholicism was no secret, and contemporaries reported that he spent 12 years living openly as a “papist.” This did not sit well with the English crown, which was concerned that English Catholics would choose loyalty to the pope over loyalty to the king. Jonson and his fellow Catholics thus lived under a constant shadow of suspicion.
      Jonson’s work came under increased scrutiny owing to his religious affiliations. The production of his play Seianus His Fall, for instance, led to accusations of “popperie and treason” from members of the Privy Council. Although there is nothing overtly Catholic in the play, its depiction of a group of virtuous individuals constantly surveilled and suspected by a morally bankrupt authority would have resonated strongly with English Catholics and infuriated the government.
      Eventually, Jonson reconverted to the Church of England in 1610, most likely after James I forbade Catholics any access to his court and insisted that they renounce the sovereignty of the pope and pledge their loyalty to him. Even then, Jonson showed an interested in Catholic beliefs and practices until the end of his life.
